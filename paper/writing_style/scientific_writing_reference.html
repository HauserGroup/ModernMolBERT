I

Writing Workflow & Strategy

Two distinct phases with different objectives: the outline resolves what to argue; the manuscript resolves how to say it. The most expensive revision cycles happen when both problems are being solved simultaneously.

The two-phase process

Phase 1 — Outline · goal: logical agreement
Build a hierarchical outline (section → subsection → point-level). Circulate to co-authors, incorporate feedback, and obtain explicit sign-off on the logical structure before any prose is written. Structural disagreements are cheap at the outline stage and very expensive after prose exists: a co-author who restructures a written section is redoing someone else's sentence-level work, not just rearranging ideas.
Phase 2 — Manuscript · goal: clear communication
Write section-by-section. Send each completed section for feedback while you start the next — a parallel review loop in which you are never idle and reviewers receive focused, bounded chunks rather than a full draft. Focus is now purely on expression; content and order are already resolved. Announce section delivery dates to co-authors: the social commitment reduces drift and keeps the pipeline moving.
Why the split matters: agreeing on the argument and polishing the prose are different cognitive tasks. Mixing them means every structural change forces a rewrite of finished sentences — the single biggest source of wasted revision effort on multi-author papers.
II

Architecture & Narrative Spine

Before drafting, fix the spine of the paper: the one problem it addresses and the single logical line from question to answer. Every figure, paragraph, and discussion point should map back to it.

The focus formula — define the study's spine first

Reduce the paper to one sentence: [Variable / intervention] has an effect on [outcome] in/from [population / sample]. This Variable–Outcome–Population (VOS) triad is the diagnostic that recurs throughout the paper — it should be identifiable in the title, the aim, and the conclusion. When a figure relates to none of the three components, it likely belongs in supplementary material.

Use this triad to check whether every section stays on the same problem. All three elements should appear in the title and the conclusion.

The narrative chain — research question, aim, and conclusion must mirror each other

Write the research question, aim, and conclusion as three explicit sentences before drafting. The conclusion must be a direct, specific answer to the gap sentence in the introduction — not a paraphrase, not a broadened claim. The aim statement (which closes the introduction) is worded to match the gap and bridges the two. If you cannot trace a logical line from the gap sentence to the conclusion, the narrative is broken somewhere in between.

Research question
Whether intermittent fasting (IF) influences the axonal regenerative ability of the PNS remains to be investigated.
Aim
We investigated whether IF would prime sensory neurons for enhanced axonal regeneration and recovery in a model of sciatic nerve injury.
Conclusion
IF facilitates regeneration and functional recovery of sensory axons through a microbiome-derived metabolite, indole-3-propionic acid.

Example: Serger et al. Nature 2022; 607:585–592

The arc maps onto the paper's sections — Beginning (Introduction: set scene, introduce conflict), Middle (Results: conflict unfolds), End (Discussion/Conclusion: resolution). Editors read for this arc; structurally coherent papers are harder to reject on scope grounds.
Pitfall: conclusions that introduce ideas not present in the research question leave reviewers asking "so what did you set out to show?" Keep the conclusion's vocabulary tightly coupled to the research question's. If the conclusion sentence could stand alone as a direct answer to the question sentence, the paper is coherent.

Calibrate background depth to the journal's readership — not to your field

The same finding needs a different introduction depending on how broad the journal's readership is. This is not about simplifying; it is about where in the knowledge funnel your readers already sit. The same senior author published two meningeal-lymphatics papers within two months — one in Nature, one in Nature Neuroscience — whose openings differed substantially in assumed context.

Nature (broad audience)
Opens with the CNS as an immune-privileged organ and establishes why immune surveillance in the CNS matters before naming the meninges. Avoids jargon like "perivascular efflux."
Nature Neuroscience (specialist)
Opens directly with "lack of lymphatic drainage" as the prior claim to contest. Uses "intraparenchymal macromolecules," "arachnoid granulations," and "perineural lymphatics" without definition.
Practical signal: if you find yourself defining terms your target journal's reviewers would consider foundational, your introduction is written for the wrong audience — and the same problem in reverse if you omit context a neighboring-field reader needs. Read three recent papers in your target journal and count the sentences of background before the first cited gap. Audience dimensions to decide upfront: global vs. regional significance; broad vs. specialist readership; academic vs. clinical/policy application.
III

Introduction

The introduction follows an inverted funnel. Its goal is to give readers just enough context to understand why your specific research question is important — not a comprehensive review. Over-writing the introduction is among the most common first-submission mistakes.

Funnel architecture — and the editor-critical gap sentence

Wide context → field problem → specific gap → your aim. Every introductory paragraph should narrow scope, and the research question must emerge from the gap you identify — not be stated first and justified afterwards (a common inversion that reads as cherry-picking the literature). The non-obvious part: editors read the gap paragraph first to assess novelty. Write it with the same care as your abstract.

introduce topic
what is known
the gap
aims
Introduce topic — why this matters, with readers in mind. For broad journals start wide; for specialist journals start with the most recent relevant claim in the field.
What is known — use up-to-date references. Cite your own prior work here to establish expertise, not to pad the reference list.
The gap (editor-critical) — the structural hinge. Signal with however, yet, despite, or remains unclear. State what is unknown, not just what you did. Write it factually, not evaluatively: "surprisingly little is known about…" invites a reviewer to ask why little is known. Prefer "whether X affects Y in Z has not been established."
Specific aims — flows from the gap. "Here we show…" or "To address this, we…" — one or two sentences only. Stating findings beyond the abstract removes the narrative tension that makes a reader continue; findings belong in the abstract and results.
The test for each paragraph: does removing it make the gap less surprising or less significant? If no, it does not belong. Over-packed introductions signal to editors that the authors have not identified what is actually novel.

Literature-review paragraph anatomy — a four-part arc, not "claim then citations"

Opening with citations instead of a claim disrupts logical flow: readers learn what a study found before they know why it is relevant. Each paragraph should follow a four-part arc.

1 — State claim
Make the scientific assertion first. "Although AD research has been neuron-centric for decades, studies have highlighted the role of non-neuronal cells…"
2 — Support with evidence
Cite studies that substantiate the claim. Outdated citations signal that your literature search stopped years ago.
3 — Interpret or extend
Synthesize, don't list. Introduce a second concept that connects to your gap. "Oligodendrocytes (~20% of brain cells) show substantial myelin changes in both patients and mouse models."
4 — Emphasize importance / pivot to gap
End with why this matters and what remains unresolved. "However, despite their vital function, it is unclear if and how oligodendrocyte fate is altered in AD."
IV

Methods

Write Methods for the reviewer who would have done it differently. The recurring failures are incomplete power calculations, citing methods without enough detail to evaluate execution, and leaving defensible analytical choices unjustified.

Power calculations must include all four components

A power calculation missing any one of these is incomplete and will flag in review:

Effect size
Clinically meaningful difference (e.g. 15% improvement)
Power
Typically 80–90%
Alpha (α)
0.05 two-sided is standard
Sample size
N resulting from the calculation

Citing vs. describing previous methods

Citation ("as previously described") is appropriate only when the technique is well-established and your readers will recognize it. When you do cite, follow with "In brief…" and a condensed description — reviewers need enough detail to evaluate execution even if they trust the method.

Justify analytical choices when alternatives exist

Whenever two valid analysis approaches exist, state why you chose yours. Reviewers who would have chosen differently need a reason not to object; without a justification, methodological objections are routine.

Preempt with validation: if you chose one analysis over another, show equivalence in supplementary/extended data (replots using the alternative). This removes the reviewer objection before it is raised.
V

Results

Results should guide the reader rather than deposit data. The governing discipline is one figure at a time, each paragraph built to a fixed internal architecture that moves from why to what to so what — while staying strictly observational.

Align the aim with the problem — match on every VOS axis

Decompose the research problem into Variable (intervention or exposure), Outcome (what is unknown and must be measured), and Sample (biological or clinical context). The aim must map onto all three with precision — naming the correct outcome domain is not sufficient if the specific phenomenon differs from the gap stated in the problem.

Problem (Simmler et al. Nature 2022)
Although preliminary studies in rodents suggest that ketamine presents a risk for addiction, the long-term effects of ketamine on mesolimbic reward circuits and behaviour remain elusive in mice.
Variable
Ketamine
Outcome
Mesolimbic circuits & addictive behaviour
Sample
Mice

Three candidate aims — same method, same sample, different outcome specification

✗…investigate whether ketamine more strongly modulates the mesolimbic or mesocortical pathways. The problem identifies a gap about mesolimbic circuits; it does not ask which pathway is stronger. A comparison aim answers a different question.
✗…investigate whether ketamine modulates the mesolimbic circuit to mediate its antidepressant effects. Antidepressant effects are not part of the stated problem. Same variable and sample, entirely different outcome phenomenon.
✓…investigate whether ketamine increases mesolimbic dopamine and assess its effects on synapses and addictive behaviour. Specifies mesolimbic dopamine as the mechanistic outcome and addictive behaviour as the functional outcome — matching the problem on both its V and O axes.

Section architecture — one figure at a time

Organize Results under clear descriptive subheadings, one per experiment cluster. Each subheading corresponds to one figure; the prose fully exhausts that figure before the next subheading begins. Mixing figure references within a paragraph forces the reader to track multiple visual contexts simultaneously — they cannot.

Clear subheading 1
Rationale → What was done → Key trends → Takeaway
All panels of Figure 1 addressed here before moving on
Clear subheading 2
Rationale → What was done → Key trends → Takeaway
All panels of Figure 2 addressed here before moving on

Results paragraph structure — the four-zone model

Each paragraph follows a fixed four-zone structure. The order reflects how readers build understanding: they need why before what, and what before so what.

① Rationale
Why this experiment follows from the prior one or from the research question. "To provide more direct support for…" / "To test whether…" This sentence links the paragraph into the paper's logical chain; a paragraph without it reads as disconnected.
② What was done + figure citation
States the approach and cites the figure. Embed brief contextual definitions inline via appositive for any non-obvious measure: "Butyrate, a major end-product of gut bacterial metabolism, was…" — the reader now understands why the molecule was measured without a methods detour. Especially important for biochemical markers specialists use routinely but others may not recognise.
③ Key trends
Direction and significance of each main outcome, with panel references. For multi-panel figures, address each cluster explicitly rather than summarising across them. Use directional keywords — significantly decreased, markedly elevated, no significant difference — to give the reader a read-key before they look at the figure.
④ Takeaway / summary
One sentence consolidating what the figure shows overall. This is a data summary, not a causal interpretation — it stays at the observational level. It can link back to other analyses ("These data support our metagenomic findings…") but must not assert mechanism.

Trend-verb vocabulary & a worked example

We examined whether hippocampal Fshr ablation mimics systemic FSH-Ab (Fig. 3). AAV2-siFshr-injected mice showed decreases in FSHR, C/EBPβ, AEP, cleaved APP, and phospho-Tau; increases in dendritic spines and synapse number; and dampened neuronal apoptosis (Fig. 3a–d). Together, these data provide clear evidence for hippocampus-resident neuronal FSHRs mediating FSH's effects on AD pathogenesis.

Xiong et al. Nature 2022; 603:470–476

Name the direction, not just "a change": decreased, increased, enhanced, dampened, abrogated, attenuated, abolished, preceded, converged, elevated. Avoid "there was a change in X." "Notably" and "importantly" mark a paragraph's single most significant finding — use at most once per Results section.

Summary vs. interpretation — where each belongs

Results — use this
"These data are consistent with a decrease in the capacity of the gut microbiome to harvest nutrients during these interventions."
Observational. Describes the pattern without asserting cause.
Discussion — not Results
"These data demonstrate that underfeeding reduces gut microbiome diversity and impairs host energy extraction."
Causal claim. Requires prior reasoning to support; belongs in Discussion.

The combined Results–Discussion paragraph — two extra zones

A common format at Nature-portfolio journals: each paragraph carries both data and interpretation. Two additional zones extend the standard Results structure, and the synthesis sentence at the close is the paragraph's highest-stakes move.

①②Rationale + what was done (as in a separate Results section)
③Key trends, panel by panel
④ Context with previous studies
Link the finding explicitly to prior published work. "Consistent with previous findings that NLGN3 induces synapse-associated gene transcription in high-grade gliomas…" This zone carries bidirectional comparisons: what your model shares with prior work, and what differs. Acknowledging open questions here signals calibrated confidence.
⑤ Synthesis / importance
Makes the inferential move — what do the combined results raise as a possibility? Uses hedging calibrated to what the evidence supports; the hedge is not timidity but an accurate signal of the inferential distance between data and claim. "Taken together, these observations raise the possibility that neuronal activity-regulated NLGN3 shedding drives the formation and growth of low-grade glioma."

Hedging vocabulary for the synthesis sentence

raises the possibility thatis consistent withtaken together, suggestsmay implyis likely due toremains a question for future worksupports the idea that
Acknowledging open questions explicitly — "Whether X also occurs in Y remains a question for future work" — strengthens rather than weakens the paragraph. It signals that you understand the limits of your data, which reviewers read as a marker of rigour.

Figure captions vs. Results text — different jobs, no duplication

Captions and Results text serve strictly different functions and should not duplicate each other. When they do, the reader reads the same information twice and gains nothing the second time.

Figure caption
Factually presents the data and analyses: what each panel measured, comparison groups, imaging modalities, scale bars, n values, and statistical tests. A reader should understand the experiment from figure + caption alone.
Results text
Describes the trends and patterns the author wants noticed; provides inline context for non-obvious measures. Does not re-list panel contents — the caption did that. Tells you what to see, not what was done.

Caption anatomy

Caption titleStates the result, not the approach. "Targeted Fshr knockdown ameliorates AD neuropathology and impaired spatial memory" — not "Effects of Fshr knockdown on AD." Method-describing titles give the reader nothing before they open the figure.
Per-panel dataOne descriptive entry per labelled panel: what was measured, comparison groups, technical detail required to read the panel (imaging modality, indicator markers). Scale bars go here.
Additional infon values per panel as discrete numbers, data presentation format (mean ± s.e.m.), statistical test(s), and explicit one- or two-sided confirmation. Required for reproducibility and audited by editors.

Reporting summaries & compliance

Mandatory for life sciences, behavioural & social sciences, and ecological/evolutionary/environmental research at Nature-portfolio journals. Submitted during peer review and published alongside the accepted manuscript — not internal only. Field-specific variants exist (solar cells, lasers, etc.); check the target journal's author guidelines.

Statistics
Exact n as discrete numbers (not ≈20 or "several"). All tests named with one/two-sided confirmation. Effect sizes (Cohen's d, Pearson's r) with how they were calculated. For Bayesian analyses: priors and Markov Chain Monte Carlo settings. For complex designs: the appropriate correction level for multiple tests.
Data availability
Accession codes, unique identifiers, or working links provided at submission. Any access restrictions explicitly stated. Clinical or third-party datasets require a policy-compliant availability statement even when data cannot be shared.
Code & software
Custom code central to the main claims must be available to editors and reviewers on request. Best practice: deposit in a community repository with a persistent identifier (GitHub + Zenodo for a DOI). Editors may decline a manuscript if critical code is unavailable and obstacles cannot be justified.
Materials & systems checklist
Every material category declared — including a negative response if not applicable (n/a). Antibodies: supplier, catalogue number, validation references (with PMIDs), and dilution. Animals: strain, source, age, sex, housing conditions.
VI

Discussion

The Introduction mapped existing knowledge and identified the gap; the Results introduced new findings. The Discussion shows how the new piece fits the existing puzzle — and what the fit means for the field. Results introduce; the Discussion contextualizes and expands, moving from specific (your work) toward broad (field consequences) in a triangle that widens toward implications.

What the Discussion must do

Commonest editor critique (Lu Shi, Sr. Editor, Nature Nanotechnology): "The author failed to connect the interpretation of their findings in response to the research objective that they proposed in the introductory part." The Discussion must close the loop the Introduction opened — answering, specifically, the gap the Introduction posed.

Audience calibration — context and implications move in opposite directions

Two calibration decisions shape the Discussion: how much context to provide, and how broadly to draw implications. Both pivot on the study's aims — context funnels down toward what the study directly addresses; implications expand outward from what it directly demonstrated.

Context ▽
Broad-readership journals need the field-level stakes restated; specialist journals do not — assume the reader knows why the question matters. Unnecessary context signals a mismatch between paper and venue.
Implications △
Broad-readership journals reward implications drawn outward to clinical relevance or cross-disciplinary application; specialist journals expect implications grounded narrowly in the subfield. Drawing too broadly for a specialist venue reads as overreach.
Writing the same Discussion for a general and a specialist journal is a common resubmission error. Recalibrate both context depth and implication scope when switching targets.

Opening the Discussion — three beats

Re-introduce topicfield-level importance statement; can invoke a paradigm shift or scale problem
Re-state the problemecho the Introduction gap: "Although X was demonstrated… Y has yet to be achieved"
Summarize achievements"Here, we…" signals the paper's direct contribution; summarizes what was done and found
Single-cell transcriptomics enables systematic characterization of cellular diversity in the brain, allowing a paradigm shift in neuroscience from anatomical to molecular classification of cell types. Although dense sampling of mouse cortex demonstrated great cellular diversity, similar coverage of human cortex has yet to be achieved. Here, similar dense sampling defined 75 cell types in human MTG, achieving robust cell typing despite increased biological and technical variability across individual human brains.

Hodge et al. Nature 2019; 573:61–68

Body-paragraph architecture — four layers, data before interpretation

Each substantive Discussion paragraph follows four layers. Opening with a stated finding before its interpretation anchors the claim in data — interpretations unmoored from a finding read as speculation. The synthesis sentence should not merely restate the finding but make explicit what it collectively demonstrates or settles.

1 · Summarize key findingsone to two sentences; what the data showed
2 · Interpretationwhat this likely means; hedged; can offer multiple competing explanations
3 · Context with previous studieshow this aligns with, extends, or contradicts prior literature
4 · Synthesize to highlight valuewhat this collectively confirms, demonstrates, or enables
Subpopulations that progressed faster on MDS-UPDRS Part III did not progress faster on Parts II and I. This may suggest that motor sign progression precedes notable changes in both motor and nonmotor symptoms. A difference in clinical rating versus patient self-awareness of motor symptoms can also explain this discrepancy12. Much longer studies may be required to test disease-modifying treatments on patient-reported symptoms. Moreover, this confirms that motor signs remain the most reliable endpoint of disease progression in early-stage PD, as also shown in studies on prodromal PD13.

Pagano et al. Nat Med 2024; 30:1096–1103

The layer sequence is logical, not mechanical — context can appear at both layers 2 and 4, as above. What must not shift: data before interpretation, interpretation before synthesis. Interleaving citations at the interpretation step is appropriate when prior work motivates the specific inference offered.

Negative results — report plainly, reason analytically

Transparent reporting of negative results prevents duplication of failed approaches, generates testable hypotheses, and strengthens the scientific record. The negative finding should open the paragraph plainly — not buried in a subordinate clause or softened by qualification. The body is analytical reasoning, not apology; multiple competing explanations, each individually hedged, are expected. False certainty about why something failed is worse than offering several possibilities.

Report negative resultstated plainly at the opening; do not soften with syntactic burial
Interpretationmultiple plausible explanations, each hedged; cite supporting evidence per explanation
Generate hypotheseswhat future experimental design would test or resolve the finding
Impact on study validityexplicitly addresses whether and how this limits conclusions
We did not observe objective responses in this cohort of heavily treated patients. Several factors may account for the low response rate, including insufficient T cell expansion, lack of antigen specificity, and/or tumor cells not being PD-L1 dependent. While all enrolled patients had PD-L1⁺ tumors, PD-L1 expression by immunohistochemistry does not necessarily correlate with PD-L1 dependency. The lack of tumor response could be related to T cell anergy or exhaustion from chronic antigen exposure28. Future clinical trials should use improved quantities of tumor-reactive or tumor antigen-specific T cells.

Lu et al. Nat Med 2020; 26:732–740

Limitations — convert a vulnerability into a demonstration of depth

A well-written limitations paragraph demonstrates technical mastery: it shows the author understands the constraints of their own experimental system well enough to reason about them with specificity. It follows the same four-layer pattern, with one key difference from a negative result — limitations require establishing that the author has engaged seriously with the published evidence bearing on the constraint. Reviewers will raise limitations not addressed; raising them first converts a vulnerability into a demonstration of depth.

Report limitationstated plainly; both real and perceived limitations warrant inclusion
Establish expertise through evidencediscuss published evidence bearing on the limitation; demonstrate command of the constraint
Generate future directionswhat experimental work would resolve or circumvent the limitation
Impact on validitywhether this constrains the generalizability of the conclusions
Unilateral and unimodal optogenetic stimulation sustained over 30s does not represent a physiological mode of LC–NA system operation. Exact firing patterns of LC neurons under environmental challenges are unknown due to the difficulty of recording large populations in freely moving animals. The highest-density recordings to date suggest highly variable, predominantly asynchronous firing — only 15% of neurons showed synchronous phasic discharge even to noxious stimuli58. It is conceivable this fraction might increase in vivo where multimodal stress signals converge and accumulate over hours. A challenge for future work will be deciphering the natural firing properties of LC neurons over longer timescales and refining stimulation protocols to closely resemble naturally occurring activity patterns.

Grimm et al. Nat Neurosci 2024

Hedging vocabulary — epistemic accuracy, not diffidence

Interpretations in the Discussion are inferences from data, not conclusions the data directly prove. The key practice is to separate the observation (unhedged data) from the interpretation (introduced by a hedging modal or verb). Over-hedging produces vagueness; under-hedging overclaims — both are editorial liabilities.

For interpretations
This may suggest that…
Likely reflects…
It is possible that…
It is conceivable that…
…could be related to…
…might increase in vivo…
For recommendations
Future studies should examine…
…may be required to test…
A challenge will be to…
These findings suggest that…
…warrants further investigation.
The separation pattern: state the data-level finding without a hedge, then introduce the inference explicitly. "Motor sign progression was faster than symptom progression. This may suggest that measurable motor changes precede patient-perceptible symptoms." The phrase "this may suggest" is the switch from observation to inference — and a signal that the interpretive register has changed.
VII

Conclusion, Title & Keywords

The conclusion, title, and keywords are the highest-leverage surfaces of the paper: the conclusion is the last passage a reader retains, and the title and keywords determine whether the paper is found at all.

The conclusion answers the problem — it does not enumerate or speculate

The conclusion's job is to directly resolve the problem stated in the introduction — not to enumerate findings (that is a results summary) and not to speculate on implications (that belongs in the discussion). The introduction's problem frame is the scoring rubric.

The stated problem (introduction)
Although microglia have been shown to participate in motor neuron degeneration in ALS, the contributions of peripheral macrophages in ALS pathogenesis remain unclear.
A — three-finding enumeration · results summary, not a conclusion
…gene modulation of the ROS pathway in peripheral macrophages reduced activation, delayed symptoms, and increased survival. Three findings in parallel — the signature of a results section. Doesn't address the stated gap at all.
B — therapeutic implication · oversteps the data
…modulating macrophages accessible at the periphery could lead to a new therapeutic strategy to treat ALS. Speculative "could lead to" is future-work language. The data support a mechanistic claim, not a therapeutic one.
C — solution to the stated problem ✓
…peripheral macrophages can influence disease progression, and their modulation can improve survival in an ALS mouse model. Directly answers the gap — do peripheral macrophages contribute? Yes. Claim scoped to the actual data (mouse model).

Four functional components of the conclusion paragraph

The conclusion is the last passage a reader encounters and the one most likely to be retained. Design it around four components. The main conclusion is not a summary of findings — it is the answer to the question the Introduction posed. Key findings are selective (1–2), not exhaustive. Implications name the immediate, concrete contribution. The final forward-looking sentence shows how this study shapes what the field does next — distinct from implications, which name what the field can now understand.

Main conclusionthe solution to the problem; directly answers the Introduction's question
Key findings1–2 most important specific results; resist listing everything
Implicationswhat the field can now understand or do that it couldn't before
Thought leadershipforward-looking statement: how this shapes the field's next questions
In summary, we provide a comprehensive dataset of cell-type-specific age-associated genes and pathways in the human OFC. We identify inhibitory LAMP5⁺LHX6⁺ neurons as transcriptionally most affected by aging; numerous gradual age-related changes overlap on the cell-type level with changes observed in AD. Additionally, we pinpoint genes with opposite regulation as potential therapeutic targets, and find evidence for accelerated transcriptomic aging in psychiatric disorders at the single-cell level. We envision that these data will provide a starting point for furthering our understanding of the aging process and the development of new therapeutic targets for aging-associated pathologies.

Fröhlich et al. Nat Neurosci 2024; 27:2021–2032

Effective titles — the three-component formula

Dropping any one of the three components forces the reader to open the abstract just to understand scope — friction that costs citation events. All three should be identifiable at a glance, in under 20 words.

Participants, tissue, model

Declarative vs. non-declarative

Declarative titles include an active verb and state the direction of effect — the reader knows the result without opening the abstract. Non-declarative titles (outcomes of variable in population) are conventional in clinical medicine, where the study design is appended as a subtitle, but they leave effect direction ambiguous.

Declarative (basic science)

Variable [active verb] Outcome in Population

PGBD5 promotes site-specific oncogenic mutations in human tumors
Effect direction is immediate: PGBD5 promotes, not merely "affects".
Non-declarative (clinical)

Outcomes of Variable in Population: study design

Cardioprotective and prognostic effects of remote ischaemic preconditioning in patients undergoing CABG: a randomised controlled trial
Effect direction unknown from title alone — accepted convention in clinical journals.

What to avoid

Questions or clever titles
A question omits the answer. A pun or cultural reference ("Charming penguins strike back") sacrifices information for wit. Neither survives a database search.
Vague scope (missing outcome)
"Evaluation of PGBD5 in human tumors" has Variable + Population but no Outcome. The reader cannot know what was measured without opening the abstract.
Abbreviations
Abbreviations block discoverability in database queries and exclude readers outside the immediate subfield at first contact.
"New" or "novel"
Circular by definition — all published work is novel. These words consume word budget the result should occupy. Hard ceiling: 20 words.

Keywords — 2–3-word phrases, calibrated by search volume

"Keywords" means 2–3-word phrases in practice. Single-word terms produce orders-of-magnitude more false matches, drowning your article in a result set no reader will sort through. The goal is a phrase specific enough to be diagnostic for your topic, broad enough to match how researchers actually search.

Identification strategy

1Generate 15 candidates from three sources: MeSH controlled vocabulary, keyword sections of 5–10 closely related published papers, and field-specific review articles.
2Test each phrase in PubMed over a 5-year window. Target a result count large enough to show active activity in the field, small enough that your article could realistically surface near the top. No universal magic number — calibrate to field breadth and whether the topic is international or regional.
3Submit 8–10, spread across specificity levels — a few broader terms for cross-disciplinary discoverability, several narrow phrases for precision matching with core readers.
Keywords that duplicate words already in the title add no discoverability value — databases index the title separately. Use keyword slots for synonyms, related subfield terms, and broader categorical concepts your title doesn't contain.
VIII

Figures & Data Visualization

Figures are read before the text and remembered after it. Two failure modes dominate: image manipulation that crosses from presentation into misrepresentation, and graph choices that encode a claim the data do not support. The governing principle throughout — the way you display data is itself a scientific assertion, subject to the same scrutiny as any sentence in the Results.

Image processing — the line between presentation and fabrication

Adjustments are permitted when they are linear (applied to the whole image) and disclosed. The moment an adjustment is local, selective, or non-linear in a way that alters the data a reader would extract, it becomes manipulation — regardless of intent.

After Schmied & Jambor, F1000Research 2021, 9:1373 — a practical image-integrity workflow.
Linear onlyBrightness, contrast, and gamma may be changed only across the entire image, never on a selected region. Adjusting one band, one cell, or one quadrant differently from the rest is manipulation even if no pixel is "added".
Preserve originalsAcquire at maximum bit depth, archive the untouched original, and process a copy. Save working files as TIFF; JPEG re-compression discards data irreversibly with every save. Journals increasingly request the raw acquisition file.
Disclose in legendIf any adjustment was made, say so in the figure legend. "Brightness and contrast were adjusted linearly across the entire image" is a one-line insurance policy against a misconduct query years later.
Annotate & inset deliberatelyScale bars, arrows, and labels belong on a separate layer so the underlying image stays untouched. Insets magnifying a region must state the magnification and not be silently brightness-matched to the parent.
Colorblindness — design for ~8% of male readers. Red/green encodings are invisible to the most common form of color vision deficiency. Use colorblind-safe pairs (magenta/green or cyan/red) for two-channel fluorescence, and verify the figure with a simulator such as Color Oracle before submission. Never let color be the only carrier of meaning — pair it with shape, position, or label.

Immunoblots & gels — the most scrutinized panels in biology

Blot and gel panels are where misconduct screens concentrate, because splicing and selective contrast are both easy and consequential. The standards below are now enforced at acceptance by most life-science journals.

~5 band-widths marginShow roughly five band-widths above and below each band. A tightly cropped band hides whether neighbouring lanes contradict the claim and whether the antibody is specific; generous margins demonstrate there is nothing to hide.
Same-gel controlsLoading controls must come from the same gel. A housekeeping band run on a different gel cannot validate equal loading. If the membrane was stripped and reprobed, state it explicitly.
Demarcate splicingLanes assembled from non-adjacent positions, or from different gels, must be separated by a visible white line or gap. Undeclared splicing is a retraction trigger.
Retain uncroppedNature Portfolio and most journals require the full uncropped image, with molecular-weight markers visible, supplied at acceptance and published in the Supplementary Information.
Provide the full uncropped blot at acceptance, not only when queried — it is faster to assemble before submission than to reconstruct under an integrity investigation.

Line art & schematics — tool choice signals craft

Schematics are judged on visual literacy. The tool leaves fingerprints, and editors recognize them.

Avoid: PowerPoint native shapes BioRender free tier — not licensed for publication Adobe Illustrator Affinity Designer Inkscape (free, vector) AJE / professional illustration services
Design for orientationA schematic should orient the reader to the experiment at a glance — flow left-to-right or top-to-bottom, group related elements, and keep one visual grammar (one arrow style, one box style) across all panels.
Vector, not rasterBuild line art as vector (SVG/PDF/EPS) so it stays crisp at any print size. Rasterized diagrams pixelate and signal amateurism.
Licensing is realBioRender's free tier explicitly forbids publication use; submitting those exports risks a copyright query at proof stage. Confirm the license before you rely on any asset library.

Graph type is a scientific claim, not a style choice

Each graph type encodes an assumption about the data's structure. Choosing the wrong one asserts a relationship that may not exist — and can mislead the authors themselves, not just readers.

Match the geometry to the data structure. The geometry is the claim.

The cautionary case — a line graph asserting a trend that isn't there

Misleading: connecting unordered categories

Manyanga et al. (BMC Public Health 2014) plotted obesity prevalence across countries as a line graph, producing a visible "downward trend". But the x-axis was a set of unordered countries, not a continuous or time variable — the line implies a progression that does not exist. Reordering the countries would invert the "trend" entirely.

A line connects points the reader is invited to interpolate between. If interpolation is meaningless, the line asserts a relationship the data cannot support — the authors were misled by their own figure.

Data-to-ink ratio — Tufte's discipline

Maximize the proportion of ink that encodes data; delete everything that doesn't. Gridlines, heavy borders, background fills, drop shadows, and redundant legends are chartjunk — they compete with the signal. A clean figure is not a stylistic preference; it lowers the cognitive cost of extracting the result.

Bar graphs fail for continuous data

The bar-graph problem (Weissgerber et al., PLoS Biology 2015)
Zone of irrelevanceA bar fills the entire range from zero to the mean, drawing the eye to a region where no data points exist. The reader's attention is anchored to empty space.
Within-the-bar biasReaders systematically judge points plotted inside the bar as "more likely to belong" than identical points above it — a documented perceptual distortion.
Hidden distributionsBimodal data, outliers, and unequal n all vanish behind a mean ± SE bar. Two radically different datasets can produce identical bars. Show the points — dot plots, box, or violin — for any continuous measurement.
Y-axis must start at zero for bar graphs — non-negotiable. A bar's length is its quantity; truncating the axis multiplies apparent differences. (Spreadsheet defaults frequently violate this — Excel will auto-truncate; the JCI and others reject truncated-axis bars outright.) Axis truncation can be defensible for line/scatter where position, not length, carries meaning — but label the break explicitly.

Three further encoding errors

3-D effectsPerspective distorts area and length, making comparison impossible. There is no legitimate use of a 3-D bar or pie in a results figure.
Pie charts for comparisonHumans compare angles poorly. A pie works only for "parts of a single whole" at a glance; for comparing magnitudes across categories, use a bar.
Qualitative adverbs for quantitative effects"Markedly increased" is not a number. Replace with the effect size and its uncertainty — e.g. "increased by 38.5% (p = 0.015)". The adverb hides whether the effect is meaningful.

Data organisation — three tiers, planned before collection

Decide what lives in the main text, what goes to Supplementary, and what becomes machine-readable Source Data — and decide it before you collect, because retrofitting the structure at submission is where authors discover their raw files were never organized to support it.

Main carries the argument; Supplementary validates; Source Data makes every plotted point recomputable.
Source Data, per panelProvide the numbers behind each graph as a spreadsheet keyed to the figure — e.g. a workbook with tabs labelled "Source Data Fig 4a, c, d". Nature journals (Nature Biomedical Engineering, Nature Cell Biology, and others) require this for primary quantitative figures.
Raw ≠ processedSource Data is the values plotted. Raw acquisition (sequencing reads, microscopy stacks, flow files) goes to a domain repository. Keep both; they answer different reproducibility questions.
Plan the architecture firstName files and folders to a panel-mapped scheme on day one. Authors who improvise this at submission routinely cannot reconstruct which CSV produced which bar — and that is the moment an editor asks.
Archive in two formatsStore data as both an open format (CSV) and the native instrument format. Check the target journal's data-availability policy before submission — requirements differ sharply by publisher and data type.
IX

Sentence-Level Craft

The architecture decides whether the paper is sound; the sentences decide whether it is readable. These techniques are mechanical and portable — they apply identically to a grant, a cover letter, or a Results paragraph. The single highest-leverage move is controlling where in the sentence information lands.

Stress & topic positions — the most underused control in scientific prose

Readers grant two positions special weight: the topic position (the opening — what the sentence is "about", linking back to prior context) and the stress position (the end — what the reader remembers and carries forward). Placing your most important information anywhere else buries it. The same words, reordered, can reverse a sentence's emphasis entirely.

Same content, opposite emphasis
version AThe board approved the raise because revenue had recovered in the third quarter.
version BBecause revenue had recovered, the board approved the raise.
topic — what the sentence is about
stress — what the reader remembers

Version A leaves the reader holding the third quarter; version B leaves them holding the raise. Neither is wrong — but only one matches what you want emphasized. Decide, then position deliberately.

Chaining: stress → topic builds momentum

Across consecutive sentences, the information you place in the stress position of one sentence should become the topic position of the next. This "old-to-new" linkage is what makes a paragraph feel like an argument rather than a list. A worked four-sentence chain on a tumor-antagonist finding:

Stress → topic chain · word counts kept tight and even
13 wordsTumors evade immune clearance by exploiting a checkpoint receptor on T cells.
13 wordsThis receptor suppresses cytotoxic activity, allowing malignant cells to proliferate unchecked.
14 wordsUnchecked proliferation generates the bulk we sought to limit with a small-molecule antagonist.
16 wordsThis antagonist restored T-cell killing and reduced tumor volume by 60% in treated mice.
Each sentence opens on the previous sentence's payload. The reader is never asked to hold a thread across a gap — the thread is handed forward explicitly. Note the even, tight length (13–16 words): the chain carries the momentum, not long sentences.

Filler — delete, don't soften

Two operations. First, phrases that can be deleted entirely with zero loss of meaning. Second, phrases that should be replaced by a single word.

Delete entirely

It should be mentioned that
As is well known,
In order to → to
It is interesting that
Needless to say,
Replace with one word
due to the fact that → because
a large number of → many
in the event that → if
at this point in time → now
has the ability to → can
in close proximity to → near

Nominalizations — turn the buried verb back into a verb

A nominalization hides an action inside a noun, then needs a weak verb (conduct, perform, carry out, make) to prop it up. The presence of those weak verbs is the diagnostic tell. Releasing the verb shortens the sentence and restores agency.

Before — 24 words

The weak-verb tell: if a sentence leans on conducted, performed, carried out, undertook, made, or provided + a noun, the real verb is almost always hiding in that noun. Pull it out.

Precision — say the specific thing

Quantify vague comparators"minimal overlap" is an opinion; "<10% of CpG sites were differentially methylated in both datasets" is a result. If you have the number, the number replaces the adjective.
Resolve ambiguous pronounsA pronoun must point to exactly one antecedent. In "the arachnoidea borders the CSF, and it regulates solute exchange," name the agent — the arachnoidea or the CSF — rather than forcing the reader to guess.
Ration subjective evaluatorsWords that tell the reader how to feel — interestingly, surprisingly, notably, strikingly, remarkably — carry weight only if rare. Budget roughly one per paper. Used on every result, they signal nothing and read as padding.
Stacks two weaknesses in one clause

"It is interesting to note that expression was significantly reduced."

"It is interesting to note that" is deletable filler and a subjective evaluator; "significantly" without a p-value or effect size is an unquantified claim. The repaired sentence states the effect size and lets the reader judge whether it is interesting.

Active voice — the default, with three principled exceptions

Aim for roughly 75% active / 25% passive. Active voice names the agent, shortens the sentence, and assigns responsibility — which is why AMA, APA, Chicago, IEEE, and Nature all now recommend it as the default. Passive is a deliberate tool for three specific jobs, not a register to write in by habit.

1 · Agent is irrelevant or obviousIn Methods, who performed a step rarely matters. "Samples were processed blinded to genotype" keeps focus on the procedure. Compare the needless active "models were evaluated by our team" — the agent ("our team") adds nothing.
2 · Avoiding false agency / causationPassive can prevent overclaiming. "The Mediterranean diet lowered LDL" asserts causation an observational design can't support; "lower LDL was observed in the Mediterranean-diet group" reports the association without the causal claim.
3 · Maintaining the topic threadWhen the stress→topic chain requires a given noun to open the sentence, passive may be the only way to put it there. "Economic growth was driven by the reforms" keeps economic growth in the topic slot if that is the running thread — active would force the reforms to the front.

Sentence length — deliberate variation, 10–20 words

Hold most sentences in the 10–20 word range, and vary deliberately: a short sentence after several long ones lands a point; uniform length — even uniformly short — produces monotony that tires the reader regardless of how strong the content is. Length is a rhythm control, not a quota.

As the chief editor of Nature Cancer has observed, two of the most common reasons a paper reads as less concise than it is are unnecessary repetition and habitually long sentences — both are rhythm failures before they are content failures.

Diagnostic: read a paragraph aloud. Where you run out of breath, the sentence is too long; where the cadence flattens, the lengths are too uniform.

Connectors — choose by logical relation, then rotate within the category

A connector is a promise about the logical relationship to what came before. Using one that names the wrong relation (a contrast word where the logic is additive) actively misleads. First pick the category the logic demands; then rotate within it so the same word doesn't repeat and flatten the prose.

Contrast

Similarity
similarly · likewise · in the same way · correspondingly · equally
Addition
moreover · furthermore · in addition · also · beyond this · what is more

The error is not repetition of a word so much as repetition that flattens logical signposting: if every paragraph opens with "However," the reader stops registering contrast as meaningful. Rotate within the correct category; never reach across categories for variety.

Latinate display vocabulary signals effort, not rigor. Plain equivalents read faster and translate better — Nature's guidance explicitly favors plain words for an adjacent-discipline and non-native-English readership. This is not dumbing down; it is removing a comprehension tax.

Reporting Compliance & AI Policy

Two compliance regimes now sit at the submission gate: structured reporting of statistics, data, code, and materials (covered in Part V), and disclosure of any AI involvement in the writing. Both are checked before review in a growing number of journals, and both have produced retractions where authors got them wrong.

AI / LLM disclosure — by publisher

The policies converge on two non-negotiables — an LLM cannot be an author, and a human is accountable for every word — but differ on where and whether use must be disclosed. Verify the target journal's current policy before submission; these are revised frequently.

ElsevierDisclose AI use in the manuscript; the disclosure statement appears in the published work.
Science journalsThe strictest: report the full prompt, tool name, and version in the Methods section, and additionally note use in the cover letter and acknowledgements.
Springer Nature (revised)AI-assisted copy editing (grammar, style, readability) no longer requires disclosure; generative content creation still does. Springer Nature also prohibits generative-AI images and asks peer reviewers not to upload manuscripts to AI tools.
UniversalHuman accountability for the final text is required in all cases — responsibility cannot be attributed to an LLM, which is why an LLM can never be listed as an author.
Documented failure — Boder et al., Radiology Case Reports 2024

The published article contained a verbatim LLM refusal, printed mid-sentence: "…the management of bilateral iatrogenic — I'm very sorry, but I don't have access to real-time…"

The paper was retracted primarily for missing patient consent; the undisclosed AI use was flagged separately as a policy breach that peer review failed to catch. The visible artifact is the tell — but the consent failure was the substantive violation. Both passed review.

LLM-assisted editing — what it does well, and where it fails

Used deliberately, an LLM reliably improves sentence-level clarity and grammar. It improves paragraph structure only when given enough context to infer the section's purpose. Without that context, it fixes surface problems and leaves the underlying organisation untouched.

A useful prompt names three things

1Who you are — field and role. "I am a neuroscience researcher…"
2Context — the section and its purpose. "…writing the introduction of an article on [X]"
3The task — the specific ask. "Improve the clarity and grammar of the following paragraph."
No context

"Please improve the following paragraph:" → grammar and readability improve, but the paragraph stays one block. The model has no basis to split it by logical function (topic → problem → agents).

With context

"I am a biomedical researcher writing the introduction of an article on [topic]. Improve the clarity and grammar of…" → grammar, readability, and structure improve; a run-on becomes three paragraphs with distinct roles.

Critical limits — treat all AI text as an unverified first draft. LLMs hallucinate: independently verify every number, citation, and mechanistic claim against primary sources. Zhang et al. (Surfaces and Interfaces, 2024) was retracted in part because the introduction was published beginning "Certainly, here is a possible introduction for your topic:" — proof the generated text was pasted without being read, let alone verified.
XI

Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Principles

1 · The stress→topic chain is the most mechanically specific technique in the entire corpus
Most writing advice is directional ("be concise", "be active"). The Gopen & Swan reader-expectation model is different: it tells you exactly where in a sentence to put information and how to thread sentences together. It doubles as a debugging tool — when a paragraph reads as a disconnected list, the fix is almost always that one sentence's stress payload was not carried into the next sentence's topic slot. It is the highest-leverage, most teachable, and most consistently underused move in scientific prose.
2 · Graph-type choice is a reproducibility issue, not an aesthetic one
A bar graph hiding a bimodal distribution, or a line connecting unordered categories, doesn't just look wrong — it encodes a claim the data cannot support, and the first people misled are usually the authors themselves. The Manyanga obesity "trend" and the Weissgerber bar-graph critique are the same lesson from two directions: the geometry you choose is an inference. Treat figure design with the scrutiny you give a statistical test.
3 · Source data and unprocessed images must be architected before collection, not at submission
The per-panel Source Data file, the uncropped blot with visible markers, the raw-vs-processed distinction — none of these can be reconstructed after the fact if the files were never organized to support them. The recurring failure mode is discovering at acceptance that the data architecture the journal now requires was never built. Name files to a panel-mapped scheme on day one; the cost of doing it early is trivial against the cost of doing it under an integrity query.
4 · The original materials assumed clean results — Parts V and VI now close that gap
The source Masterclass content is strongest on papers with tidy, confirmatory findings and offers little for the messier reality of mixed, negative, or unexpected results. That gap is now filled: the negative-results framing (report what the absence rules out), the limitations construction (state the constraint, then its bounded consequence), the aim–problem alignment diagnostic, and the hedging vocabulary (calibrate certainty to evidence) together give the tools for findings that don't resolve cleanly. Real datasets rarely do; this consolidated reference is built to handle that.
5 · "Interestingly" is a per-paper budget, and most writers overspend it on line one
Subjective evaluators (interestingly, surprisingly, notably, strikingly) are the cheapest possible way to tell a reader something matters — which is exactly why they stop working when overused. Rationed to roughly one per paper, the word genuinely flags the result that broke your prior. Sprayed across every paragraph, it reads as filler and trains the reader to ignore it. Spend it once, on the finding that earned it.
Cross-cutting · One discipline unifies the whole manuscript: make the claim and its support legible at every scale
The same principle recurs from title to pixel. The VOS title makes the contribution legible in 20 words; the gap paragraph makes it legible in the introduction; the four-zone Results paragraph makes each finding legible in sequence; the figure makes the data legible at a glance; the stress position makes it legible in a single sentence. A manuscript fails not usually because the science is weak but because the contribution is illegible at one of these scales. Every technique in this reference is, ultimately, a legibility instrument — and the VOS Outcome slot, the gap sentence, and the stress position are the three places it most often breaks.
