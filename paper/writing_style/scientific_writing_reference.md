## I. Writing Workflow & Strategy

Two distinct phases with different objectives: the outline resolves *what* to argue; the manuscript resolves *how* to say it. The most expensive revision cycles happen when both problems are being solved simultaneously.

### Two-phase process

**Phase 1 — Outline (resolve the argument).** Decide the single problem the paper addresses, the logical line from question to answer, and which figures carry the claim. Work at the level of bullet points and figure sketches. No prose. The deliverable is a defensible skeleton: if a colleague reads the outline and the argument holds, you are ready to draft.

**Phase 2 — Manuscript (resolve the expression).** Only once the skeleton is fixed do you write sentences. Drafting prose before the argument is settled means rewriting that prose every time the argument shifts — the costliest possible order of operations.

> The recurring, expensive mistake is collapsing the two phases: writing polished paragraphs around an argument that is still moving. Settle the structure first; the structure is cheap to change as bullet points and ruinous to change as paragraphs.

## II. Architecture & Narrative Spine

Before drafting, fix the spine of the paper: the one problem it addresses and the single logical line from question to answer. Every figure, paragraph, and discussion point should map back to it.

### The focus formula (VOS) — the contribution in one line

The whole paper resolves to a relationship between three elements. Naming them explicitly, before drafting, is the single most clarifying planning act.

| Element | Question it answers | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Variable / Intervention** (V) | What are you investigating? | Intermittent fasting |
| **Outcome(s)** (O) | What are you measuring? | Axonal regeneration |
| **Population / Sample** (P) | Where are measurements from? | Damaged peripheral nervous system |

Read as: *Variable* has an effect on *Outcome* in or from *Population*. This same V→O→P triple recurs in the title (Part VII) and in the aim–problem alignment of the Results (Part V); they are the same idea expressed at different scales.

### The narrative chain — question → aim → conclusion

The paper is one logical arc, mapped onto beginning, middle, and end:

| Stage | Role | Lives in |
|---|---|---|
| **Research question** | the problem | Introduction (beginning) |
| **Aim** | how you address it | bridges into the work (middle) |
| **Conclusion** | the answer | Discussion (end) |

If the conclusion does not answer the research question, the arc is broken — and no amount of sentence polishing repairs a broken arc.

### Journal calibration — a pre-drafting strategic decision

The entry-point journal determines how much background the reader needs, how broad the framing must be, and what counts as a sufficient advance. This is decided *before* drafting, not adjusted afterward, because it changes the introduction's width and the discussion's reach.

**Worked contrast — the same finding, two journals.** A study of meningeal lymphatics:

- **For *Nature* (broad):** frame against the general problem of how the brain clears waste; the reader is a non-specialist who needs the stakes established from first principles.
- **For *Nature Neuroscience* (specialist):** frame against the specific open question in CNS fluid dynamics; the reader already knows why the system matters and wants the precise advance.

The result is identical; the introduction that sells it is not. Choosing the target first lets you write the correct introduction once instead of three times.

## III. Introduction

The introduction follows an inverted funnel. Its goal is to give readers just enough context to understand why your specific research question is important — not a comprehensive review. Over-writing the introduction is among the most common first-submission mistakes.

### The funnel

1. **Broad context** — the general area and why it matters (1–2 sentences, calibrated to journal breadth).
2. **Narrowing** — what is known in the specific subfield.
3. **The gap** — the specific thing that is *not* known. This is the hinge of the whole introduction.
4. **This study** — your aim, stated as the response to the gap.

### The gap paragraph — the structural hinge editors read most critically

The gap is where the introduction earns or loses the reviewer. It must state precisely what is unknown and why that unknown matters — not merely that "more research is needed". A vague gap ("the mechanism remains poorly understood") signals you have not located your own contribution.

**Editor-critical test:** the gap sentence and your aim sentence must be mirror images. If the gap is "it is unknown whether X regulates Y," the aim must be "we tested whether X regulates Y." A mismatch here is the fastest way to read as unfocused.

### The literature-review paragraph — a four-part internal arc

Each background paragraph is itself a miniature argument, not a citation dump:

1. **Topic sentence** — what this paragraph is about.
2. **What is known** — the established findings, synthesized (not listed paper by paper).
3. **The tension or limit** — where the existing knowledge stops or conflicts.
4. **Bridge** — how this sets up the next paragraph or the gap.

Worked on Alzheimer's/oligodendrocyte biology: open on the cell type's established role, synthesize the consensus, surface the unresolved conflict in the literature, then bridge to the specific unknown your study addresses. The reader should feel pulled toward your gap, not buried in prior work.

## IV. Methods

Write Methods for the reviewer who would have done it differently. The recurring failures are incomplete power calculations, citing methods without enough detail to evaluate execution, and leaving defensible analytical choices unjustified.

### Power calculation — state all four components

An incomplete power statement is unverifiable. A complete one names:

1. **Effect size** — the magnitude you powered to detect, with its source (pilot data, prior literature).
2. **Significance level (α)** — conventionally 0.05, stated explicitly.
3. **Power (1 − β)** — conventionally 0.80 or higher.
4. **Resulting sample size** — the n the first three imply.

Reporting only "n = 12, sufficiently powered" gives the reviewer nothing to check. All four components make the calculation reproducible.

### Citing vs. describing methods

Citing a method is acceptable *only* when the cited source contains enough detail to execute it, and you changed nothing. The convention is **"In brief, …"**: cite the source, then summarize the essential steps so the reader can evaluate execution without chasing the reference. Any deviation from the cited protocol must be described in full — a modified method is a new method.

### Justify analytical choices; preempt with validation

Wherever a defensible alternative existed — a different normalization, threshold, model, or exclusion rule — state why you chose yours. Unjustified choices read as arbitrary and invite the reviewer to suspect you tried several and reported the favorable one. Where possible, preempt the objection with a validation (a sensitivity analysis, a positive control, a robustness check) rather than waiting to defend it in rebuttal.

## V. Results

Results should guide the reader rather than deposit data. The governing discipline is one figure at a time, each paragraph built to a fixed internal architecture that moves from *why* to *what* to *so what* — while staying strictly observational.

### Aim–problem alignment (VOS)

Before writing Results, confirm the aim is stated at the right grain. The aim must name the same Variable, Outcome, and Population the figures actually address — no broader, no narrower.

**Worked example — a ketamine study (after Simmler et al.).** Three candidate aims for the same data:

- *"To understand depression"* — too broad; the figures cannot deliver this.
- *"To measure ketamine's effect on synaptic density in mouse prefrontal cortex"* — correctly scoped; V (ketamine), O (synaptic density), P (mouse PFC) all map to real panels.
- *"To measure dendritic spine counts in layer-5 neurons at 24h"* — too narrow; it describes one panel, not the paper.

The middle version is the aim the Results can honour. Misalignment here propagates into every downstream section.

### One figure at a time

Each Results paragraph governs exactly one figure (or one coherent panel group). The reader processes the argument in the order you present the evidence; interleaving figures forces them to hold multiple threads and is the most common reason Results read as a data dump.

### The four-zone Results paragraph

A fixed internal architecture, applied to every figure:

1. **Rationale** — why this experiment, given what came before (one sentence, links to the prior result).
2. **What was done** — the approach, briefly (the reader has Methods for detail).
3. **Trends / what was found** — the observation, with the numbers.
4. **Takeaway** — what this result establishes (observational, not interpretive — no mechanism, no field-level claim).

**Worked example (after Xiong et al., on *Fshr*).** Open on why the receptor was the next thing to test; state the manipulation; report the directional result with effect size; close on what it observationally establishes — without yet claiming why. Trend verbs (*increased, declined, accumulated, shifted*) carry the finding; reserve causal verbs for the Discussion.

> **Summary vs. interpretation:** Results *summarize* what was observed; the Discussion *interprets* what it means. A causal verb ("ketamine restored connectivity") in the Results section asserts an interpretation the design may not support. Keep Results to what a second observer would agree they saw.

### Combined Results & Discussion — a five-zone paragraph

When a journal merges the sections, each paragraph extends the four-zone structure with a fifth interpretive zone:

1. Rationale → 2. What was done → 3. Trends → 4. Takeaway (observational) → **5. Interpretation** (mechanism, fit with literature, hedged appropriately).

Worked on an NLGN3/glioma finding: the first four zones stay strictly observational; only zone 5 advances the mechanistic reading, and it is hedged to match the strength of the evidence (*these data suggest…, consistent with…*).

### Figure captions vs. Results text

The caption and the Results text do different jobs and must not duplicate:

- **Caption** — makes the figure self-contained: what each panel shows, what the symbols/colors/error bars mean, n, statistical test. A reader who skips the text should still understand the figure.
- **Results text** — narrates the *argument* the figure supports; it does not re-describe the axes.

**Caption anatomy:** title phrase → per-panel description → definitions (symbols, abbreviations, error bars) → statistics (test, n, what significance markers denote).

### Reporting summaries — four checklist domains

Most journals now require structured reporting before review. Plan for all four:

| Domain | What must be reported |
|---|---|
| **Statistics** | Tests used, exact n per group, what error bars represent, exact p-values (not just "<0.05"), corrections for multiple comparisons. |
| **Data** | Availability statement; repository accession; what is in main vs. supplementary vs. source data. |
| **Code** | Availability of analysis code; environment/version; repository link. |
| **Materials** | Reagents, cell lines (with authentication), antibodies (with catalogue/RRID), organisms, key resources. |

## VI. Discussion

The Introduction mapped existing knowledge and identified the gap; the Results introduced new findings. The Discussion shows how the new piece fits the existing puzzle — and what the fit means for the field. Results *introduce*; the Discussion *contextualizes and expands*, moving from specific (your work) toward broad (field consequences) in a triangle that widens toward implications.

### The Discussion pyramid (narrow → broad)

1. **Summarize what you did** — topic, gap, achievements (brief; do not re-report Results).
2. **Interpret your findings** — what they mean, mechanism, fit and friction with prior work.
3. **Why it matters to the field** — the broad implication, the widest point of the triangle.

> **Editor critique (after Lu Shi):** the most common Discussion failure is an inverted or flat pyramid — restating results at length and never reaching the field-level "so what". The reviewer is asking "why should the field care?"; if the widest tier is missing, the paper reads as a report rather than a contribution.

### Audience calibration — context (▽) vs. implications (△)

Calibrate the two ends to the reader. A broad journal needs more **context** at the opening (▽, wider) and a carefully widened **implications** close (△); a specialist journal needs less context and can reach a sharper, more technical implication. The shape of the triangle is set by who is reading.

### Opening the Discussion — three beats

The first paragraph (after Hodge) does three things in sequence: **(1)** restate the central finding in one plain sentence; **(2)** state what it answers relative to the gap; **(3)** preview the interpretation to come. No suspense — tell the reader the conclusion immediately.

### Discussion body — the four-layer paragraph (fractal)

Each body paragraph (after Pagano) runs: **rationale → method → result → takeaway** — the same four-layer logic as the Results paragraph, now operating at interpretive scale. This structure is *fractal*: it works at the sentence, paragraph, and section level. Recognizing it once lets you deploy it everywhere.

### Negative, partial, and mixed results

A negative result is informative when it rules something out. Report what the absence of effect *excludes*, not merely that nothing reached significance. (After Lu et al., 2020.) Frame it as a constraint on the hypothesis space — "X does not drive Y under these conditions, which narrows the candidate mechanisms to…" — rather than as a failed experiment. *This addresses a gap the original Nature Masterclass materials left open: how to structure a paper whose findings do not resolve cleanly.*

### Limitations — state the constraint, then bound its consequence

A limitation (after Grimm) is two clauses, not one: name the constraint, then state its *bounded* consequence. "Our cohort was single-centre **(constraint)**, which limits generalizability to similar care settings but does not affect the internal validity of the dose–response relationship **(bounded consequence)**." Naming a limitation without bounding it hands the reviewer an unguided weakness; bounding it shows you have already reasoned about its reach.

### Hedging vocabulary — calibrate certainty to evidence

Match the strength of the verb to the strength of the evidence. The separation pattern — strong claim, hedged mechanism — keeps you honest:

- **Strong (observed):** *show, demonstrate, establish, find* — for what the data directly support.
- **Moderate (inferred):** *suggest, indicate, support, are consistent with* — for interpretation.
- **Tentative (speculative):** *may, might, could, raise the possibility that* — for mechanism beyond the data.

Overclaiming with "demonstrate" on a correlational result is the single most common reviewer flag; under-claiming with "may suggest" on a direct measurement wastes a real finding.

## VII. Conclusion, Title & Keywords

The closing elements carry disproportionate weight per word: the conclusion is what the reader remembers, and the title and keywords decide whether they ever arrive.

### The conclusion — its job, and the A/B/C diagnostic

The conclusion answers the research question and states the contribution — it does not summarize the paper. A useful diagnostic (worked on an ALS/macrophage study) asks three questions of any candidate conclusion:

- **(A) Does it answer the original question?** If the intro asked whether macrophages drive progression, the conclusion must say whether they do.
- **(B) Is it supported by the results presented?** No claim that outruns the data.
- **(C) Does it state why it matters?** The contribution to the field, not just the local finding.

### Four-component conclusion (after Fröhlich)

1. **The answer** — to the research question, in one sentence.
2. **The evidence basis** — what the answer rests on (brief).
3. **The implication** — what it changes for the field.
4. **The outlook** — the next question or application it opens (forward-looking, not a limitations rehash).

### Effective titles — the three-component formula

A strong title is identifiable at a glance, under 20 words, and contains all three VOS elements. Dropping any one forces the reader to open the abstract just to understand scope — friction that costs citation events.

**Variable → Outcome → Population.**

**Declarative vs. non-declarative:**

| Style | Pattern | Example | Effect direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Declarative** (basic science) | Variable [active verb] Outcome in Population | *PGBD5 promotes site-specific oncogenic mutations in human tumors* | Immediate — *promotes*, not merely "affects" |
| **Non-declarative** (clinical) | Outcomes of Variable in Population: study design | *Cardioprotective and prognostic effects of remote ischaemic preconditioning in patients undergoing CABG: a randomised controlled trial* | Unknown from title alone — accepted clinical convention |

**What to avoid:**

- **Questions or clever titles** — a question omits the answer; a pun ("Charming penguins strike back") trades information for wit. Neither survives a database search.
- **Vague scope (missing Outcome)** — *"Evaluation of PGBD5 in human tumors"* has V + P but no O; the reader cannot know what was measured.
- **Abbreviations** — block discoverability in database queries and exclude readers outside the immediate subfield at first contact.
- **"New" / "novel"** — circular (all published work is novel) and they consume word budget. Hard ceiling: **20 words**.

### Keywords — 2–3-word phrases, calibrated by search volume

"Keywords" means **2–3-word phrases** in practice. Single words produce orders-of-magnitude more false matches, drowning the article in a result set no one will sort.

| Term | PubMed results (5-yr window) | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "tumor" | 819,412 | single word — unusable signal-to-noise |
| "tumor microenvironment" | 27,413 | phrase — specific enough to matter |

**Identification strategy:**

1. **Generate ~15 candidates** from three sources: MeSH controlled vocabulary, keyword sections of 5–10 closely related papers, and field-specific reviews.
2. **Test each phrase in PubMed** over a 5-year window. Target a count large enough to show field activity, small enough that your article could surface near the top. No universal magic number — calibrate to field breadth and topic scope.
3. **Submit 8–10**, spread across specificity levels — a few broader terms for cross-disciplinary reach, several narrow phrases for precision matching.

> Keywords that duplicate words already in the title add no discoverability value — databases index the title separately. Use keyword slots for synonyms, related subfield terms, and broader categorical concepts the title doesn't contain.

## VIII. Figures & Data Visualization

Figures are read before the text and remembered after it. Two failure modes dominate: image manipulation that crosses from presentation into misrepresentation, and graph choices that encode a claim the data do not support. Governing principle: **the way you display data is itself a scientific assertion**, subject to the same scrutiny as any sentence in the Results.

### Image processing — the line between presentation and fabrication

Adjustments are permitted when they are **linear** (applied to the whole image) and **disclosed**. The moment an adjustment is local, selective, or non-linear in a way that alters the data a reader would extract, it becomes manipulation — regardless of intent.

A seven-step workflow (after Schmied & Jambor, *F1000Research* 2021, 9:1373); the integrity-critical steps are marked:

| Step | Action | Integrity obligation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Acquire** | Raw, maximum bit depth; keep originals. ✱ |
| 2 | **Work on a copy** | TIFF, never JPEG; non-destructive. ✱ |
| 3 | Crop | Framing only — not to hide data. |
| 4 | Resize | Preserve aspect ratio. |
| 5 | **Adjust** | Linear, whole-image only (brightness/contrast); disclose in legend. ✱ |
| 6 | **Annotate** | Scale bar, arrows on a new layer. ✱ |
| 7 | **Export** | Flatten; ≥300 dpi; keep the layered file. ✱ |

- **Linear only** — brightness, contrast, gamma changed only across the *entire* image, never a selected region. Adjusting one band, cell, or quadrant differently is manipulation even if no pixel is "added".
- **Preserve originals** — acquire at max bit depth, archive the untouched original, process a copy. JPEG re-compression discards data irreversibly with every save. Journals increasingly request the raw acquisition file.
- **Disclose in legend** — "Brightness and contrast were adjusted linearly across the entire image" is a one-line insurance policy against a future misconduct query.
- **Annotate & inset deliberately** — scale bars, arrows, labels on a separate layer; insets must state magnification and not be silently brightness-matched to the parent.

> **Colorblindness — design for ~8% of male readers.** Red/green encodings are invisible to the most common color-vision deficiency. Use colorblind-safe pairs (**magenta/green** or **cyan/red**) for two-channel fluorescence; verify with a simulator (e.g. Color Oracle) before submission. Never let color be the *only* carrier of meaning — pair it with shape, position, or label.

### Immunoblots & gels — the most scrutinized panels in biology

- **~5 band-widths margin** — show roughly five band-widths above and below each band. A tight crop hides whether neighbouring lanes contradict the claim and whether the antibody is specific.
- **Same-gel controls** — loading controls must come from the *same* gel. A control on a different gel cannot validate equal loading. If stripped and reprobed, state it.
- **Demarcate splicing** — lanes assembled from non-adjacent positions or different gels must be separated by a visible white line or gap. Undeclared splicing is a retraction trigger.
- **Retain uncropped** — Nature Portfolio and most journals require the full uncropped image with molecular-weight markers visible, supplied at acceptance and published in the SI.

### Line art & schematics — tool choice signals craft

- **Avoid:** PowerPoint native shapes; BioRender free tier (not licensed for publication).
- **Use:** Adobe Illustrator, Affinity Designer, Inkscape (free, vector); professional services (e.g. AJE).
- **Design for orientation** — flow left-to-right or top-to-bottom, group related elements, one visual grammar (one arrow style, one box style) across all panels.
- **Vector, not raster** — build as SVG/PDF/EPS so line art stays crisp at any print size.
- **Licensing is real** — BioRender's free tier forbids publication use; confirm the license before relying on any asset library.

### Graph type is a scientific claim, not a style choice

Each graph type encodes an assumption about the data's structure; the wrong one asserts a relationship that may not exist.

| Graph type | Asserts / appropriate for |
|---|---|
| **Line** | Continuous, *ordered* x-axis (time, dose). The line invites interpolation between points. |
| **Bar** | Counts / discrete categories. Y-axis must start at zero. |
| **Scatter** | Relationship between two continuous variables. |
| **Box** | Distribution summary (median, quartiles, range). |
| **Violin** | Distribution + density — reveals shape (bimodality) a box hides. |
| **Pie** | Parts of a single whole, at a glance — use sparingly; humans compare angles poorly. |

**Cautionary case — a line graph asserting a trend that isn't there.** Manyanga et al. (*BMC Public Health* 2014) plotted obesity prevalence across countries as a **line graph**, producing a visible "downward trend". But the x-axis was a set of *unordered countries*, not a continuous or time variable — the line implies a progression that does not exist, and reordering the countries would invert the "trend" entirely. A line connects points the reader is invited to interpolate between; if interpolation is meaningless, the line asserts a relationship the data cannot support. The authors were misled by their own figure.

**Data-to-ink ratio (Tufte).** Maximize the proportion of ink that encodes data; delete the rest. Gridlines, heavy borders, background fills, drop shadows, and redundant legends are *chartjunk* — they compete with the signal. A clean figure lowers the cognitive cost of extracting the result.

**Bar graphs fail for continuous data (Weissgerber et al., *PLoS Biology* 2015):**

- **Zone of irrelevance** — a bar fills the range from zero to the mean, drawing the eye to a region where *no data points exist*.
- **Within-the-bar bias** — readers judge points plotted inside the bar as "more likely to belong" than identical points above it (a documented perceptual distortion).
- **Hidden distributions** — bimodality, outliers, and unequal n vanish behind a mean ± SE bar; two different datasets can produce identical bars. Show the points (dot plot, box, or violin) for any continuous measurement.

> **Y-axis must start at zero for bar graphs — non-negotiable.** A bar's length is its quantity; truncating the axis multiplies apparent differences. (Spreadsheet defaults frequently violate this — Excel auto-truncates; the *JCI* and others reject truncated-axis bars outright.) Axis truncation can be defensible for line/scatter where position, not length, carries meaning — but label the break explicitly.

**Three further encoding errors:**

- **3-D effects** — perspective distorts area and length; no legitimate use in a results figure.
- **Pie charts for comparison** — use a bar to compare magnitudes across categories.
- **Qualitative adverbs for quantitative effects** — "markedly increased" is not a number. Replace with effect size and uncertainty, e.g. *"increased by 38.5% (p = 0.015)"*.

### Data organisation — three tiers, planned before collection

Decide what lives in the main text, what goes to Supplementary, and what becomes machine-readable Source Data — **before** you collect, because retrofitting the structure at submission is where authors discover their raw files were never organized to support it.

| Tier | Holds | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Main figure / table** | Results supporting the central claim | Does it carry the argument? |
| **Supplementary** | Validation / extension, not central | Does it support but not carry the claim? |
| **Source Data** | The numeric values *behind* each plotted panel | Machine-readable, per panel |
| **Repository / archive** | Raw acquisition (reads, stacks, flow files) | CSV + native format |

- **Source Data, per panel** — provide the numbers behind each graph as a spreadsheet keyed to the figure, e.g. tabs labelled *"Source Data Fig 4a, c, d"*. Required by *Nature Biomedical Engineering*, *Nature Cell Biology*, and others for primary quantitative figures.
- **Raw ≠ processed** — Source Data is the values *plotted*; raw acquisition goes to a domain repository. Keep both; they answer different reproducibility questions.
- **Plan the architecture first** — name files/folders to a panel-mapped scheme on day one. Authors who improvise at submission routinely cannot reconstruct which CSV produced which bar — the moment an editor asks.
- **Archive in two formats** — open format (CSV) and native instrument format. Check the journal's data-availability policy *before* submission; requirements differ sharply by publisher and data type.

## IX. Sentence-Level Craft

The architecture decides whether the paper is sound; the sentences decide whether it is readable. These techniques are mechanical and portable. The single highest-leverage move is controlling *where* in the sentence information lands.

### Stress & topic positions — the most underused control in scientific prose

Readers grant two positions special weight: the **topic position** (the opening — what the sentence is "about", linking back to prior context) and the **stress position** (the end — what the reader remembers and carries forward). Placing key information anywhere else buries it. The same words, reordered, reverse the emphasis.

**Same content, opposite emphasis** (topic in *[brackets]*, stress in **bold**):

- Version A: *[The board approved the raise]* because revenue had recovered in **the third quarter**.
- Version B: *[Because revenue had recovered]*, the board approved **the raise**.

Version A leaves the reader holding *the third quarter*; version B leaves them holding *the raise*. Decide which you want emphasized, then position deliberately.

**Chaining: stress → topic builds momentum.** The information in the stress position of one sentence should become the topic position of the next. This "old-to-new" linkage is what makes a paragraph read as an argument rather than a list. A four-sentence chain (stress payload → next topic), with tight, even length:

1. *(13 words)* Tumors evade immune clearance by exploiting **a checkpoint receptor on T cells**.
2. *(13 words)* *[This receptor]* suppresses cytotoxic activity, allowing **malignant cells to proliferate unchecked**.
3. *(14 words)* *[Unchecked proliferation]* generates the bulk we sought to limit with **a small-molecule antagonist**.
4. *(16 words)* *[This antagonist]* restored T-cell killing and reduced tumor volume by 60% in **treated mice**.

Each sentence opens on the previous sentence's payload; the reader is never asked to hold a thread across a gap. The chain carries the momentum — not long sentences.

### Filler — delete, don't soften

**Delete entirely (zero loss of meaning):** *It is important to note that · It should be mentioned that · As is well known · It is interesting that · Needless to say* — and *In order to* → **to**.

**Replace with one word:** *due to the fact that* → **because** · *a large number of* → **many** · *in the event that* → **if** · *at this point in time* → **now** · *has the ability to* → **can** · *in close proximity to* → **near**.

### Nominalizations — turn the buried verb back into a verb

A nominalization hides an action inside a noun, then needs a weak verb (*conduct, perform, carry out, make*) to prop it up. Those weak verbs are the diagnostic tell.

| Nominalized | → | Released verb |
|---|---|---|
| an estimate of | → | **estimate** |
| made a decision | → | **decided** |
| confirmation of | → | **confirm** |
| a correlation with | → | **correlated with** |

**Worked example (24 → 12 words):**

- *Before:* "We conducted an assessment of the expression of TREM2 and performed a comparison of the levels between the two groups."
- *After:* "We assessed TREM2 expression and compared levels between the two groups."

> **The weak-verb tell:** if a sentence leans on *conducted, performed, carried out, undertook, made,* or *provided* + a noun, the real verb is hiding in that noun. Pull it out.

### Precision — say the specific thing

- **Quantify vague comparators** — "minimal overlap" is an opinion; "<10% of CpG sites were differentially methylated in both datasets" is a result. If you have the number, the number replaces the adjective.
- **Resolve ambiguous pronouns** — a pronoun must point to exactly one antecedent. In *"the arachnoidea borders the CSF, and it regulates solute exchange,"* name the agent rather than forcing the reader to guess.
- **Ration subjective evaluators** — *interestingly, surprisingly, notably, strikingly, remarkably* carry weight only if rare. Budget roughly **one per paper**.

**Stacked-weakness example:** *"It is interesting to note that expression was significantly reduced."* — "It is interesting to note that" is deletable filler *and* a subjective evaluator; "significantly" without a p-value or effect size is an unquantified claim. The repaired sentence states the effect size and lets the reader judge whether it is interesting.

### Active voice — the default, with three principled exceptions

Aim for roughly **75% active / 25% passive**. Active names the agent, shortens the sentence, and assigns responsibility — which is why **AMA, APA, Chicago, IEEE,** and **Nature** all recommend it as the default. Passive is a deliberate tool for three jobs:

1. **Agent is irrelevant or obvious** — in Methods, who performed a step rarely matters. *"Samples were processed blinded to genotype"* keeps focus on the procedure; the active *"models were evaluated by our team"* adds nothing with "our team".
2. **Avoiding false agency / causation** — *"The Mediterranean diet lowered LDL"* asserts causation an observational design can't support; *"lower LDL was observed in the Mediterranean-diet group"* reports the association without the causal claim.
3. **Maintaining the topic thread** — when the stress→topic chain needs a given noun to open the sentence, passive may be the only way to put it there. *"Economic growth was driven by the reforms"* keeps *economic growth* in the topic slot if that is the running thread.

### Sentence length — deliberate variation, 10–20 words

Hold most sentences in the **10–20-word** range and *vary* deliberately: a short sentence after several long ones lands a point; uniform length — even uniformly short — produces monotony that tires the reader regardless of content strength. Length is a rhythm control, not a quota. As the chief editor of *Nature Cancer* has observed, two of the most common reasons a paper reads as less concise than it is are unnecessary repetition and habitually long sentences — both are rhythm failures before they are content failures.

> Diagnostic: read a paragraph aloud. Where you run out of breath, the sentence is too long; where the cadence flattens, the lengths are too uniform.

### Connectors — choose by logical relation, then rotate within the category

A connector is a promise about the logical relationship to what came before. Pick the *category* the logic demands, then rotate *within* it so the same word doesn't repeat and flatten the prose.

| Relation | Connectors (rotate within) |
|---|---|
| **Contrast** | however · whereas · by contrast · conversely · nonetheless · yet · although |
| **Similarity** | similarly · likewise · in the same way · correspondingly · equally |
| **Addition** | moreover · furthermore · in addition · also · beyond this · what is more |
| **Result** | therefore · consequently · as a result · thus · hence · accordingly |

> The error is not repetition of a word so much as repetition that *flattens logical signposting*: if every paragraph opens with "However," the reader stops registering contrast as meaningful. Rotate within the correct category; never reach across categories for variety.

### Vocabulary reduction — plain words, deliberately chosen

Latinate display vocabulary signals effort, not rigor. Plain equivalents read faster and translate better — Nature's guidance explicitly favors plain words for an adjacent-discipline and non-native-English readership. This is not dumbing down; it removes a comprehension tax.

| Ornate | → | Plain | | Ornate | → | Plain |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ameliorate | → | Improve | | Modality | → | Way, approach |
| Commence | → | Begin, start | | Nascent | → | Emerging |
| Corroborate | → | Confirm, validate | | Obfuscate | → | Hide, confuse |
| Elucidate | → | Explain, clarify | | Paradigm | → | Theory, model |
| Endeavour | → | Try, aim | | Ramification | → | Consequence |
| Exacerbate | → | Worsen, heighten | | Superfluous | → | Unnecessary |
| Facilitate | → | Help, enable, aid | | Utilise | → | Use |

**Worked example (after Chini et al., *Aging Cell* 2024):**

- *Before:* "We observed a precocious manifestation of spontaneous carcinogenesis in the knockout cohort."
- *After:* "We observed early development of spontaneous tumors in the knockout cohort." — shorter, unambiguous, loses no precision.

> **Manchester Academic Phrasebank** (phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk) — a free, function-organized bank of vetted academic phrasings (describing methods, reporting results, signalling limitations). The reference of choice when a sentence's *content* is clear but its conventional *phrasing* is not, and a useful scaffold for non-native English writers.

## X. Reporting Compliance & AI Policy

Two compliance regimes sit at the submission gate: structured reporting of statistics, data, code, and materials (Part V), and disclosure of any AI involvement in the writing. Both are checked before review in a growing number of journals, and both have produced retractions.

### AI / LLM disclosure — by publisher

The policies converge on two non-negotiables — **an LLM cannot be an author**, and **a human is accountable for every word** — but differ on *where* and *whether* use must be disclosed. Verify the target journal's current policy; these are revised frequently.

| Publisher | Disclosure requirement |
|---|---|
| **Elsevier** | Disclose AI use in the manuscript; the statement appears in the published work. |
| **Science journals** | Strictest: full prompt, tool name, and version in the **Methods**; also note in cover letter and acknowledgements. |
| **Springer Nature** *(revised)* | AI-assisted *copy editing* (grammar, style, readability) **no longer requires disclosure**; generative content still does. Prohibits generative-AI images; asks reviewers not to upload manuscripts to AI tools. |
| **Universal** | Human accountability for the final text in all cases — responsibility cannot be attributed to an LLM, which is why an LLM can never be an author. |

**Documented failure — Boder et al., *Radiology Case Reports* 2024.** The published article contained a verbatim LLM refusal printed mid-sentence: *"…the management of bilateral iatrogenic — I'm very sorry, but I don't have access to real-time…"*. The paper was retracted primarily for missing patient consent; the undisclosed AI use was flagged separately as a policy breach that peer review failed to catch. The visible artifact is the tell — but the consent failure was the substantive violation. Both passed review.

### LLM-assisted editing — what it does well, and where it fails

Used deliberately, an LLM reliably improves sentence-level clarity and grammar. It improves *paragraph structure* only when given enough context to infer the section's purpose; without that, it fixes surface problems and leaves the organisation untouched.

**A useful prompt names three things:**

1. **Who you are** — field and role. *"I am a neuroscience researcher…"*
2. **Context** — the section and its purpose. *"…writing the introduction of an article on [X]"*
3. **The task** — the specific ask. *"Improve the clarity and grammar of the following paragraph."*

**What context changes:**

- *No context* — *"Please improve the following paragraph:"* → grammar and readability improve, but the paragraph stays one block; the model has no basis to split it by logical function (topic → problem → agents).
- *With context* — *"I am a biomedical researcher writing the introduction of an article on [topic]. Improve the clarity and grammar of…"* → grammar, readability, **and structure** improve; a run-on becomes three paragraphs with distinct roles.

> **Critical limits — treat all AI text as an unverified first draft.** LLMs hallucinate: independently verify every number, citation, and mechanistic claim against primary sources. **Zhang et al.** (*Surfaces and Interfaces*, 2024) was retracted in part because the introduction was published beginning *"Certainly, here is a possible introduction for your topic:"* — proof the generated text was pasted without being read, let alone verified.

## XI. Synthesis & Highest-Leverage Principles

**1 · The stress→topic chain is the most mechanically specific technique in the entire corpus.** Most writing advice is directional ("be concise", "be active"). The Gopen & Swan reader-expectation model tells you *exactly where* to put information and how to thread sentences. It doubles as a debugging tool: when a paragraph reads as a disconnected list, the fix is almost always that one sentence's stress payload was not carried into the next sentence's topic slot.

**2 · Graph-type choice is a reproducibility issue, not an aesthetic one.** A bar graph hiding a bimodal distribution, or a line connecting unordered categories, encodes a claim the data cannot support — and the first people misled are usually the authors. The Manyanga obesity "trend" and the Weissgerber bar-graph critique are the same lesson from two directions: the geometry you choose *is* an inference.

**3 · Source data and unprocessed images must be architected before collection, not at submission.** The per-panel Source Data file, the uncropped blot with visible markers, the raw-vs-processed distinction — none can be reconstructed after the fact if the files were never organized to support them. The recurring failure is discovering at *acceptance* that the required data architecture was never built. Name files to a panel-mapped scheme on day one.

**4 · The original materials assumed clean results — Parts V and VI close that gap.** The source Masterclass content is strongest on tidy, confirmatory findings and offers little for mixed, negative, or unexpected results. That gap is now filled: the negative-results framing (report what the absence rules out), the limitations construction (constraint + bounded consequence), the aim–problem alignment diagnostic, and the hedging vocabulary together handle findings that don't resolve cleanly. Real datasets rarely do.

**5 · "Interestingly" is a per-paper budget, and most writers overspend it on line one.** Subjective evaluators are the cheapest way to tell a reader something matters — which is why they stop working when overused. Rationed to ~one per paper, the word genuinely flags the result that broke your prior. Spend it once, on the finding that earned it.

**Cross-cutting · One discipline unifies the whole manuscript: make the claim and its support legible at every scale.** The same principle recurs from title to pixel. The VOS title makes the contribution legible in 20 words; the gap paragraph makes it legible in the introduction; the four-zone Results paragraph makes each finding legible in sequence; the figure makes the data legible at a glance; the stress position makes it legible in a single sentence. A manuscript usually fails not because the science is weak but because the contribution is *illegible* at one of these scales. Every technique here is, ultimately, a legibility instrument — and the VOS Outcome slot, the gap sentence, and the stress position are the three places it most often breaks.
